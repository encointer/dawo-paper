\begin{abstract}
Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) typically govern treasury resources through token-weighted voting, producing plutocratic outcomes in which influence scales with capital. We present a field-deployed governance architecture that combines decentralized proof-of-personhood (PoP) with on-chain governance to bound influence inequality in treasury allocation, approximating one-person-one-vote. Personhood is established through simultaneous physical key-signing ceremonies. On this foundation we implement a protocol with open proposal submission, parallel competing proposals, participation-weighted voting (capped at three), and adaptive quorum biasing. We apply this infrastructure to community-currency treasuries, enabling communities to allocate native tokens for leader compensation and manage stablecoin-backed swap options for local businesses. The architecture is operational in two small, locally cohesive African communities. We treat our empirics as observations from a low-conflict, early-stage governance environment rather than a stress test of democratic robustness: turnout is moderate, approval near-unanimous, and proposal submission concentrated in a few community leaders, surfacing knowhow asymmetry as a first-order constraint on de facto equal influence.
%
\keywords{DAO governance, proof-of-personhood, one-person-one-vote, democratic treasury management, community currencies}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) manage billions of dollars in collective treasury assets, yet their governance mechanisms almost universally default to token-weighted voting: one token, one vote. This design produces plutocratic outcomes in which financial capital determines political power~\cite{schneider2024governable}. Empirical analysis of major DAOs confirms extreme concentration: Fritsch et al.~\cite{fritsch2024analyzing} find Gini coefficients of voting power exceeding 0.98 in Compound and Uniswap governance, with fewer than 10 delegates controlling a majority of decisions. Large token holders dominate proposal outcomes, while smaller participants (often the most active community members) are systematically marginalized. Schneider identifies this as a structural consequence of the ``personhood problem'': without a reliable way to verify that each participant is a unique human, pseudonymous blockchain systems cannot implement democratic alternatives.

Proof-of-personhood (PoP) protocols offer a path beyond this impasse. By establishing that each participant in a governance process is a distinct human being, PoP enables governance mechanisms that decouple political influence from financial stake, even if (as in our system) some bounded participation-based weighting is retained. Established democracies routinely deviate from strict one-person-one-vote in bounded ways: federal upper chambers (the Swiss \emph{St\"anderat}, the US Senate) assign equal seats per sub-jurisdiction regardless of population, electoral colleges and malapportioned districts shift effective vote weight by geography, and long-resident non-citizens are typically excluded from the franchise~\cite{samuels2001value}; we view our cap-of-three participation weighting as a comparable, bounded deviation that is orders of magnitude smaller than the inequalities token-weighted governance produces. Several PoP approaches have been proposed: biometric verification (e.g., iris scanning~\cite{worldcoin2023}), social graph analysis~\cite{brightid2020}, concurrent reverse-Turing tests~\cite{idena2019}, and physical ceremonies~\cite{ford2020identity}. Boog~\cite{boog2025} provides a systematic classification. Each approach involves distinct tradeoffs in privacy, accessibility, and decentralization~\cite{buterin2023biometric}.

In this paper, we present a field-deployed governance architecture that combines ceremony-based PoP with on-chain democratic governance for treasury resource allocation. Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A democratic governance protocol built on PoP, featuring participation-weighted voting (bounded by a hard cap), adaptive quorum biasing, and parallel competing proposals (Section~\ref{sec:governance}).
\item An application to treasury governance for community currencies, enabling communities to democratically allocate funds for leadership compensation and stablecoin-backed swap options (Section~\ref{sec:treasury}).
\item Empirical observations from two small African communities, covering participation, voting-power distribution, AQB dynamics, and the role of knowhow asymmetry as a de facto constraint on equal influence (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}
We position our empirics as evidence from a low-conflict, early-stage governance environment, not as validation of democratic robustness under contestation; this distinction motivates much of the discussion in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

We begin with related work (Section~\ref{sec:related}), then describe the PoP mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:pop}), the governance protocol (Section~\ref{sec:governance}), and treasury governance (Section~\ref{sec:treasury}). Section~\ref{sec:results} presents field results, followed by discussion (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) and conclusion (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=====================================================================

\paragraph{DAO Governance and Plutocracy.}
Schneider~\cite{schneider2024governable} systematically critiques token-weighted voting as producing ``persistent plutocracy'' and ``implicit feudalism.'' Fritsch et al.~\cite{fritsch2024analyzing} provide empirical evidence: voting power Gini coefficients exceed 0.98 in Compound and Uniswap, with single-holder delegates controlling over 90\% of voting power. Proposed mitigations such as quadratic voting~\cite{buterin2019flexible}, conviction voting, and delegation remain vulnerable to Sybil attacks when participants can cheaply create multiple identities.

\paragraph{Proof-of-Personhood.}
Ford~\cite{ford2020identity} distinguishes \emph{identity} (divisive attributes) from \emph{personhood} (the inalienable right to participate equally) and evaluates PoP approaches against four criteria: inclusive, equal, secure, and private. Buterin~\cite{buterin2023biometric} taxonomizes PoP into approaches based on social graphs, biometrics via general or specialized hardware, and recommends hybrid strategies combining multiple paradigms. Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} conduct a deep empirical study of Idena, a ceremony-based PoP system, finding that while it achieved \emph{de jure} Sybil resistance (filtering bots), it failed at \emph{de facto} resistance: by May 2022, 23 entities controlled approximately 40\% of accounts through puppeteering. They argue that community-level identity with subsidiarity offers structural advantages over global identity schemes, aligning with our per-community governance architecture.

\paragraph{Democratic Design for Digital Spaces.}
Schneider~\cite{schneider2024governable} proposes ``modular politics'': composable governance modules that communities can mix and match according to their needs. This framework motivates our architecture, where each community independently configures governance parameters (ceremony rewards, demurrage rates, treasury policies) while sharing the underlying PoP infrastructure. The cooperative tradition of one-member-one-vote provides historical precedent for the governance model we implement on-chain.

\section{Proof-of-Personhood via Physical Ceremonies}
\label{sec:pop}
%=====================================================================

The uniqueness foundation of our governance architecture is a decentralized proof-of-personhood (PoP) protocol based on physical key-signing ceremonies~\cite{brenzikofer2019encointer}. Unlike biometric approaches that require specialized hardware~\cite{worldcoin2023} and raise privacy and centralization concerns~\cite{buterin2023biometric}, or government-issued identity documents that exclude undocumented populations~\cite{ford2020identity}, this ceremony-based approach establishes personhood through simultaneous physical co-presence.

\paragraph{Ceremony Mechanism.}
Participants register for recurring ceremonies held at fixed intervals (currently every 10 days). At the designated time, registrants are randomly assigned to meetup locations where groups of 3--15 participants gather simultaneously. During the meetup, each participant performs pairwise cryptographic attestations with all other attendees, creating a mutual proof of physical co-presence. The assignment algorithm minimizes repeat encounters across ceremonies and limits newcomers to at most one quarter of each group, preventing rapid infiltration.

The simultaneity constraint is critical: all ceremonies worldwide occur at the same moment (local solar noon), making it physically impossible for a single person to attend multiple meetups (we exclude polar regions and the international date line). This provides Sybil resistance without requiring biometric data or centralized identity verification.

\paragraph{Sybil Resistance.}
Hoffmann~\cite{hoffmann2021security} shows that creating a Sybil identity requires controlling a majority of the attendees assigned to a single meetup. However, maintaining Sybil identities across ceremonies requires the adversary to control over 50\% of the entire network's reputable population, and the analysis identifies a \emph{friction threshold} of approximately 4.5\%: if minion recruitment costs exceed this fraction of the ceremony reward, no Sybil strategy is profitable within two years. Moreover, Sybil attacks are observable by honest participants in affected meetups, and sustained attacks erode community trust in the currency, destroying the value of any rewards gained. The puppeteering risks documented by Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} for Idena are further mitigated by the physical co-presence requirement, which raises coordination costs and social detection risk.

\paragraph{Reputation Model.}
Successful ceremony attendance earns a \emph{reputation} valid for a rolling window of $R = 5$ ceremonies. Participants hold one of three statuses: \emph{Bootstrapper} (predefined founders of the community), \emph{Reputable} (at least one successful ceremony within the reputation window), or \emph{Newbie} (no recent reputation). Bootstrappers constitute the trusted setup for each community and do not change after the first ceremony. They hold no privileges beyond guaranteed assignment to every ceremony they register for.

% unnecessary for context
% To promote an honest majority at each gathering, the protocol requires that at least 2/3 of participants hold reputable or bootstrapper status. Newbies are assigned only to remaining seats under this constraint. To accelerate growth, each reputable can endorse up to two newbies per cycle, guaranteeing them assignment to the next ceremony. Reputation is non-transferable and decays automatically: a participant who stops attending loses all reputation within $R$ cycles. This creates a \emph{liveness} requirement that continuously validates personhood, unlike one-time identity verification schemes.

\section{Democratic Governance Protocol}
\label{sec:governance}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Open Proposal Submission}
\label{sec:proposals}

Any participant with reputable status can submit a governance proposal on-chain without requiring approval from existing authorities. This permissionless design prevents gatekeeping, a common failure mode in DAO governance where proposal submission is restricted to token holders above a threshold, effectively silencing smaller stakeholders~\cite{schneider2024governable}.

Each proposal specifies a \emph{ProposalAction} drawn from a fixed set of governance operations: modifying community parameters (nominal income, demurrage rate, ceremony locations), spending treasury funds, issuing swap options, or submitting non-binding petitions. Proposals are either community-scoped (only members of that community vote) or global (all communities vote). The electorate is computed at submission time and remains fixed throughout the proposal's lifetime, ensuring a stable approval threshold.

\subsection{Parallel Competing Proposals}
\label{sec:parallel}

Multiple proposals of the same type can coexist simultaneously. For parameter-setting actions (e.g., two competing proposals to set different demurrage rates), the first proposal to reach confirmed approval supersedes all older competing proposals, which transition to a \emph{SupersededBy} state. This first-to-pass mechanism prevents deadlocks where conflicting proposals block each other indefinitely.

Non-parameter actions (treasury spends, swap option grants, and petitions) do not supersede each other, allowing multiple independent spending proposals to proceed in parallel. This distinction reflects the difference between mutually exclusive parameter choices and additive resource allocations.

\subsection{Participation-Weighted Voting Power}
\label{sec:voting-power}

A strict one-person-one-vote weighting is only possible with 100\% confidence in the PoP mechanism, which is not the case in our protocol. Rather than assigning equal voting power to all eligible participants, the protocol weights votes by ceremony participation history. A voter's \emph{voting power} equals their reputation count within the eligible window for that proposal, i.e., the number of ceremonies successfully attended in recent cycles. We will later analyze how well this approximates one-person-one-vote in reality.

Formally, for a proposal submitted at ceremony index $c_s$, the eligible ceremony indices are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:voting-window}
\mathcal{C} = \{c_s - R + L_p, \ldots, c_s - 2\}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the reputation lifetime (5 ceremonies) and $L_p$ is the proposal lifetime in cycles ($L_p = 1$ in our deployment). A voter's power is $v = |\{c \in \mathcal{C} : \text{voter attended ceremony } c\}|$, capped at $|\mathcal{C}| = R - 1 - L_p = 3$.

The upper bound of the eligible window ($c_s - 2$) excludes the most recent ceremony. This prevents last-minute Sybil infiltration: an attacker cannot register new identities and immediately deploy them in a governance vote. The lower bound ensures that the oldest eligible reputations still exist at the end of the proposal lifetime, guaranteeing a stable electorate throughout the voting period.

The total electorate $E$ is the sum of reputation counts across all eligible ceremonies for the relevant community (or globally, for global proposals):
\begin{equation}
E = \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \text{reputationCount}(c)
\end{equation}

This design rewards sustained participation: a voter attending all eligible ceremonies has 3$\times$ the influence of a voter attending only once. The cap at $3\times$ bounds inequality, in contrast to token-weighted voting where power differences can span orders of magnitude. Inactive participants lose voting power automatically as their reputations expire, keeping the electorate current.

Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} found that Idena's analogous mechanism (one-pool-one-vote, which discounted influence for concentrated account pools) was decisive in enabling the protocol to pivot away from captured governance. Our participation weighting serves a similar function: it makes sustained, genuine engagement a prerequisite for governance influence.


\subsection{Adaptive Quorum Biasing}
\label{sec:quorum}

A fixed majority threshold (e.g., 50\%) creates a legitimacy problem under variable turnout: a proposal passing with 51\% of a 5\% turnout represents support from only 2.5\% of the electorate. Conversely, requiring high absolute turnout causes governance paralysis when participation fluctuates.

We adopt \emph{adaptive quorum biasing} (AQB), first introduced in the Polkadot governance design~\cite{burdges2020overview}, with a positive turnout bias. Let $E$ denote the electorate size, $T$ the total voting power of all voters (turnout), and $A$ the voting power of aye voters. The proposal passes if:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:aqb}
\frac{T - A}{\sqrt{T}} < \frac{A}{\sqrt{E}}
\end{equation}
which yields an approval threshold as a fraction of turnout:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:threshold}
\theta(T, E) = \frac{\sqrt{E}}{\sqrt{E} + \sqrt{T}}
\end{equation}

At full turnout ($T = E$), the threshold is exactly $50\%$; a simple majority suffices. As turnout decreases, $\theta$ increases toward $100\%$, requiring near-unanimity among the few who vote. This encodes a reasonable democratic principle: decisions affecting the entire community should require broad consensus when most members are absent, but a simple majority suffices when everyone participates.

Additionally, a \emph{MinTurnout} floor of 5\% of the electorate prevents proposals from passing when turnout is negligibly small, regardless of unanimity among voters.

A proposal that meets the AQB threshold enters a \emph{Confirming} state. It must remain continuously passing for a confirmation period before transitioning to \emph{Approved} and entering the enactment queue. If the threshold is no longer met (i.e., due to new nay votes), the proposal returns to \emph{Ongoing}. If a proposal is not approved within its lifetime, it is \emph{Rejected}. \section{Treasury Governance for Community Currencies}
\label{sec:treasury}
%=====================================================================

Each community maintains a treasury funded by external subsidies and transaction fees. The governance protocol enables the community to allocate these funds through democratic proposals. The simplest mechanism is a \emph{SpendNative} proposal, which pays out native blockchain tokens (KSM) from the treasury to a designated beneficiary upon approval. Communities have used this mechanism to compensate community leaders who organize ceremonies, onboard new participants, and maintain the local currency network.

Beyond direct spending, the treasury governance addresses a recurring sustainability challenge specific to community currencies: popular businesses that attract many customers accumulate tokens faster than they can spend them within the local network. Ruddick~\cite{ruddick2022grassroots} documents this ``accumulation problem'' across over 100 community currency deployments in Kenya, where businesses with excess tokens lose motivation to continue accepting the currency, threatening network viability.

\paragraph{Swap Options as Democratic Policy.}
Our architecture addresses this through \emph{treasury swap options}: a community can democratically vote to grant a specific business the right to exchange accumulated community currency tokens for stablecoins (e.g., USDC) held in the community treasury. Each swap option is defined by:
\begin{itemize}
\item A beneficiary address (the business receiving swap rights)
\item An allowance (maximum stablecoin amount available for swap)
\item An exchange rate (community currency units per stablecoin unit)
% these are not actively used:
%\item A burn flag (whether swapped community currency is burned or returned to treasury)
%\item Optional validity window (start and end dates)
\end{itemize}

To exercise a swap option, the beneficiary sends community currency tokens to the treasury and receives stablecoins up to their remaining allowance. Each exercise reduces the allowance, preventing unlimited extraction. The swapped community currency is permanently destroyed, reducing circulating supply.

\paragraph{Safeguards Against Treasury Depletion.}
Two community-level conventions protect treasuries from excessive extraction. First, a \emph{per-beneficiary limit} caps each swap option at 10\% of the unallocated treasury balance, ensuring no single business can drain a disproportionate share. Second, a 2\% \emph{exercise fee} is deducted from each swap, creating a cost of extraction that discourages speculative or unnecessary swaps. The choice of parameters has been negotiated with our communities and is subject to change.

The critical design insight is that \emph{the community itself} decides who receives swap access and under what terms. Unlike centralized models where an administrator or foundation controls treasury outflows, every swap option must pass the adaptive quorum biasing threshold described in Section~\ref{sec:quorum}. This creates democratic accountability: businesses must maintain community trust to continue receiving swap access.

We hypothesize that a community treasury need not maintain full backing for community currency turnover. If external subsidies flow into the treasury at a rate $R$ per month, the community currency can sustain a local turnover of $N \cdot R$ in real value, where $N$ depends on the velocity of circulation and the fraction of transactions that ultimately require stablecoin exit. This resembles a fractional reserve model, though applied at the community rather than banking level. The analogy to the WIR complementary currency system in Switzerland is instructive: Stodder~\cite{stodder2009} shows that endogenous credit creation in complementary currencies acts as a countercyclical stabilizer, with the effective money multiplier depending on velocity and network structure. Whether this holds for small-scale community currencies with external stablecoin reserves remains an open empirical question that this paper does not attempt to settle. We position the swap-option mechanism here as a governance instrument with an empirical track record, not as a validated monetary design.

Of the 74 proposals that received at least one vote, the mean turnout was 25.1\% of the electorate (weighted by voting power) and the mean approval rate among voters was 96.7\%. Of the seven rejected proposals, five expired with zero turnout and two received minimal voting (one to three votes total) without clearing the AQB threshold; no proposal in the dataset has been actively voted down with nay votes exceeding the threshold. Examining the 32 approved swap option proposals more closely, we find that 25.4\% of aye voters had previously transacted with the beneficiary business (i.e., sent community currency to the beneficiary address before casting their vote), suggesting that the majority of approval votes are not driven by direct client relationships but rather by community solidarity or deference to proposal submitters.

These outcomes are best read as evidence from a low-conflict, early-stage governance environment rather than as validation of the mechanism under stress. The combination of near-unanimous approval, expiry-only rejections, and small homogeneous electorates is consistent with cooperative coordination among engaged peers; it does not, however, demonstrate that AQB performs well when proposals are genuinely contested. We consider the absence of contested votes a limitation, not a result: the mechanism has not been tested against active opposition, strategic abstention, or adversarial agenda-setting, and our deployments are unlikely to generate such conditions at current scale and stakes.

\subsection{Knowhow Asymmetry and Agenda-Setting}
\label{sec:knowhow}

\paragraph{Submission concentration and informal influence.}
Of 79 proposals, only 25 distinct accounts submitted any proposal at all, and the top two submitters together account for 42\% of submissions; by contrast, 127 unique accounts have cast votes, a substantially broader base but still under a third of active ceremony participants. Proposal submission is therefore concentrated in a small subset of community leaders who also act as governance educators. This asymmetry of know-how creates an informal influence channel the protocol does not model: members with less technical understanding tend to follow the opinion of those who explain the system. In some cases, participants hand over their phones so that a single person can submit votes on their behalf. Although this does not violate one-person-one-vote at the protocol level (each participant remains custodian of their wallet), it erodes voter \emph{agency} and structurally resembles the puppeteering dynamic Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} identified as the central failure mode of Idena's governance. We observed the same pattern in pilot Leu Z\"urich in Switzerland: even when devices were not handed over, votes tracked the argumentation of the presumably \emph{knowledgeable} community leadership.

The gap between the protocol's democratic ideals and the social reality of facilitated participation is a limitation that cannot be resolved through mechanism design alone; it requires investment in governance literacy and accessible deliberation tools, neither of which is in scope for the chain layer. As community currencies grow in economic relevance, the cost of uninformed voting rises, which may drive greater engagement but also heighten capture risk through the same channel.

The 32 granted swap options were issued to 21 unique beneficiaries. Of these, 19 (90\%) have exercised their options at least partially across 28 successful exercise transactions. Including SpendNative proposals (24 enacted), treasury management constitutes 77\% of all governance proposals (61 of 79), making it the dominant governance activity. This aligns with Ruddick's observation~\cite{ruddick2022grassroots} that managing the accumulation problem is a central concern in community currency sustainability.

The total of approximately \$10,560 distributed over 16 months is equivalent to 5,280 ceremony rewards (\$2 each). In context, median daily earnings for informal sector workers are approximately \$2--3 in \locationC~\cite{nbs_tanzania_ilfs2014} and \locationD~\cite{nbs_nigeria_nlss2019}, placing the cumulative treasury outflow in a range meaningful for local economic activity.

The per-beneficiary limit of 10\% of treasury balance and the 2\% exercise fee have not yet been tested over a longer term. Current utilization levels suggest the mechanism is functioning as intended, providing democratic oversight of treasury outflows while enabling businesses to convert excess community currency to stablecoins.

Interviews with Nyota leadership reveal that the introduction of swap options has materially changed the perception of the community currency. Previously, participants spent tokens carelessly (e.g., sending 15 Nyota when 10 were owed); now that tokens can be converted to stablecoins, they are treated as a store of value. Emergent financial behavior has appeared: one member loaned 30 Nyota and agreed on repayment of 35, implying an informal interest rate. As the community leader summarized: ``Sometimes people don't have cash but they have Nyota!'' However, the offramp from stablecoins to local cash remains a significant obstacle, particularly for participants without bank accounts or credit cards. In PayNuq, business owners report waiting over a month for pending conversions through an agent, and the friction of converting stablecoins to mobile money limits the practical reach of the swap mechanism.

%=====================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=====================================================================

\paragraph{Democratic Governance Without Plutocracy.}
Our architecture shows that DAOs can govern shared treasury resources without token-weighted plutocracy when a reliable PoP layer is available. The ceremony-based PoP approach sidesteps the privacy and centralisation concerns of biometric systems while providing Sybil resistance that, per Hoffmann's analysis~\cite{hoffmann2021security}, is robust below the 50\% adversarial threshold, a condition easily maintained in small-to-medium communities with low ceremony rewards. The participation-weighted voting mechanism further strengthens governance integrity by rewarding sustained engagement over one-time participation.

\paragraph{Participation and Legitimacy.}
Adaptive quorum biasing provides a principled mechanism for navigating the tension between governance agility and democratic legitimacy under variable turnout. Our empirical findings show that proposals consistently achieve high approval rates among those who vote, while the AQB threshold ensures that low-turnout decisions require near-unanimity. We are careful, however, not to read this as validation of democratic robustness. In fact, our observations do not yet exercise AQB's discriminating power: every proposal that cleared the 5\% MinTurnout floor was approved by a near-unanimous margin, and the seven rejections either expired with zero votes (five cases) or had voting power below the MinTurnout floor (two cases). A simple majority rule paired with the same MinTurnout floor would have produced the same enactment/rejection outcomes across all 79 proposals. AQB's value lies in what it would prevent under conditions our deployments have not produced (close-to-majority opposition at moderate turnout), not in what it has decided so far.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The dataset is still small (79 proposals across two active communities over $\approx$16 months, with 74 receiving any votes) and no proposal has been actively contested with significant nay votes, so AQB has not been tested under controversial conditions and our setting is unlikely to produce them at current scale. The ceremony mechanism imposes a substantial participation burden and the matters voted on are technically complex, producing an informal power dynamic the on-chain model does not capture. Stakes are also small (rewards $\approx$\$2), which provides natural Sybil resistance through high effective friction but limits the stakes of any single decision; whether the architecture's properties hold as economic value grows is open. Sybil-resistance claims rest on Hoffmann~\cite{hoffmann2021security}; we have not conducted adversarial evaluation in our deployments.

\paragraph{Generalizability.}
The governance protocol is not specific to community currencies. Any DAO with a PoP identity layer could adopt the adaptive quorum biasing, participation-weighted voting, and parallel proposal mechanisms described here. The treasury swap governance application is domain-specific, but the underlying pattern (democratic oversight of resource allocation with per-beneficiary caps) applies broadly to grant programs, public goods funding, and other collective resource management scenarios.

However, the PoP mechanism itself is inherently local: it relies on geographically focused physical presence and social pressure within small communities where participants know each other. This social fabric is a feature (it provides Sybil resistance at low cost) but it limits the architecture to community-scale DAOs. Extending one-person-one-vote governance to larger, geographically dispersed organizations requires bridging trust between independent local communities. Delacour~\cite{delacour2023global} proposes a path toward this goal by constructing a trust graph between communities rather than between individuals.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We presented a field-deployed governance architecture for democratic treasury management in DAOs without plutocratic token-weighted voting.
By combining ceremony-based PoP with bounded participation weighting (capped at $3\times$), the system approximates one-person-one-vote governance.
Applied to treasury governance in community currencies, the architecture allows communities to democratically allocate native tokens for community leadership and manage stablecoin-backed swap options with per-beneficiary caps and exercise fees.
Empirical observations from deployments in two small African communities describe a low-conflict, early-stage governance environment: sustained participation, near-unanimous approval, no contested votes, and proposal submission concentrated in a small number of community leaders.
Knowhow asymmetry, rather than the on-chain mechanism, is the binding constraint on de facto equal influence in this setting.

Future work should test the architecture under conditions our deployments do not produce: contested proposals as communities grow, adversarial evaluation of PoP under non-cooperative conditions, formal modelling of treasury sustainability, and privacy-preserving voting through ring-VRF-based anonymous credentials~\cite{burdges2023ringvrf}.
Larger and more contested deployments are needed before the architecture's democratic properties can be assessed as the economic value of community currencies increases.
