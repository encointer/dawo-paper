\title{Democratic Treasury Governance Without Plutocracy:\\
Proof-of-Personhood Voting for DAO Resource Allocation}

\titlerunning{Democratic Treasury Governance Without Plutocracy}

% Double-blind: author info omitted for review
\author{}
\institute{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) typically govern treasury resources through token-weighted voting, producing plutocratic outcomes in which influence scales with capital. We present a field-deployed governance architecture that enables one-person-one-vote treasury allocation without reliance on centralized identity systems. Our approach combines decentralized proof-of-personhood (PoP) with on-chain democratic governance, where personhood is established through simultaneous, recurring physical key-signing ceremonies. Building on this identity foundation, we implement a governance protocol featuring open proposal submission, parallel competing proposals, participation-weighted voting power, and adaptive quorum biasing. We apply this democratic infrastructure to treasury swap governance in community currencies, enabling communities to democratically manage stablecoin-backed swap options for local businesses. The architecture is operational in two African communities. We present empirical findings from these deployments, analyzing participation patterns, voter turnout dynamics, and the effectiveness of adaptive quorum biasing in practice.
%
\keywords{DAO governance, proof-of-personhood, one-person-one-vote, democratic treasury management, community currencies}
\end{abstract}

%=====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) manage billions of dollars in collective treasury assets, yet their governance mechanisms almost universally default to token-weighted voting: one token, one vote. This design produces plutocratic outcomes in which financial capital determines political power~\cite{schneider2024governable}. Large token holders dominate proposal outcomes, while smaller participants---often the most active community members---are systematically marginalized. Schneider identifies this as a structural consequence of the ``personhood problem'': without a reliable way to verify that each participant is a unique human, pseudonymous blockchain systems cannot implement democratic alternatives.

Proof-of-personhood (PoP) protocols offer a path beyond this impasse. By establishing that each participant in a governance process is a distinct human being, PoP enables one-person-one-vote mechanisms that decouple political influence from financial stake. Several PoP approaches have been proposed---biometric verification~\cite{buterin2023biometric}, social graph analysis, and physical ceremonies~\cite{ford2020identity}---each with distinct tradeoffs in privacy, accessibility, and decentralization.

In this paper, we present a field-deployed governance architecture that combines ceremony-based PoP with on-chain democratic governance for treasury resource allocation. Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A democratic governance protocol built on PoP, featuring participation-weighted voting, adaptive quorum biasing, and parallel competing proposals (Section~\ref{sec:governance}).
\item An application to treasury swap governance for community currencies, enabling communities to democratically manage stablecoin-backed swap options (Section~\ref{sec:treasury}).
\item Empirical analysis from operational deployments in two African communities, including participation patterns, voting power distributions, and quorum dynamics (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

We begin with related work (Section~\ref{sec:related}), then describe the PoP mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:pop}), the governance protocol (Section~\ref{sec:governance}), and treasury swap governance (Section~\ref{sec:treasury}). Section~\ref{sec:results} presents field results, followed by discussion (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) and conclusion (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

%=====================================================================
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=====================================================================

\paragraph{DAO Governance and Plutocracy.}
The dominant governance paradigm in DAOs is token-weighted voting, where influence scales linearly with token holdings. Schneider~\cite{schneider2024governable} provides a systematic critique, arguing that this produces ``persistent plutocracy'' and ``implicit feudalism''---online spaces that default to autocratic or wealth-based control, eroding participants' capacity for democratic self-governance. Quadratic voting, conviction voting, and vote delegation have been proposed as mitigations, but all remain vulnerable to Sybil attacks when participants can cheaply create multiple identities.

\paragraph{Proof-of-Personhood.}
Ford~\cite{ford2020identity} distinguishes \emph{identity} (divisive attributes) from \emph{personhood} (the inalienable right to participate equally) and evaluates PoP approaches against four criteria: inclusive, equal, secure, and private. Buterin~\cite{buterin2023biometric} taxonomizes PoP into social-graph-based, general-hardware biometric, and specialized-hardware biometric approaches, recommending hybrid strategies that combine multiple paradigms. Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} conduct a deep empirical study of Idena, a ceremony-based PoP system, finding that while it achieved \emph{de jure} Sybil resistance (filtering bots), it failed at \emph{de facto} resistance: by May 2022, 23 entities controlled approximately 40\% of accounts through puppeteering. They argue that community-level identity with subsidiarity offers structural advantages over global identity schemes, aligning with our per-community governance architecture.

\paragraph{Community Currencies.}
Ruddick~\cite{ruddick2022grassroots} documents the evolution of community currencies from simple voucher systems to ``commitment pools''---curated, valued, and limited agreements within overlapping economic networks. Drawing on over 100 deployments across Kenya and Uganda, the work identifies the accumulation problem (popular businesses hoarding excess tokens) as a persistent sustainability challenge and describes community governance traditions including rotating elder councils and consensus-based decision-making.

\paragraph{Democratic Design for Digital Spaces.}
Schneider~\cite{schneider2024governable} proposes ``modular politics''---composable governance modules that communities can mix and match according to their needs. This framework motivates our architecture, where each community independently configures governance parameters (ceremony rewards, demurrage rates, treasury policies) while sharing the underlying PoP infrastructure. The cooperative tradition of one-member-one-vote provides historical precedent for the governance model we implement on-chain.

\section{Proof-of-Personhood via Physical Ceremonies}
\label{sec:pop}
%=====================================================================

The identity foundation of our governance architecture is a decentralized proof-of-personhood (PoP) protocol based on physical key-signing ceremonies~\cite{brenzikofer2019encointer}. Unlike biometric approaches that require specialized hardware~\cite{buterin2023biometric} or government-issued identity documents that compromise privacy and exclude undocumented populations~\cite{ford2020identity}, this ceremony-based approach establishes personhood through simultaneous physical co-presence.

\paragraph{Ceremony Mechanism.}
Participants register for recurring ceremonies held at fixed intervals (currently every 10 days). At the designated time, registrants are randomly assigned to meetup locations where groups of 3--12 participants gather simultaneously. During the meetup, each participant performs pairwise cryptographic attestations with all other attendees, creating a mutual proof of physical co-presence. The assignment algorithm minimizes repeat encounters across ceremonies and limits newcomers to at most one quarter of each group, preventing rapid infiltration.

The simultaneity constraint is critical: all ceremonies worldwide occur at the same moment (local solar noon), making it physically impossible for a single person to attend multiple meetups. This provides Sybil resistance without requiring biometric data or centralized identity verification.

\paragraph{Sybil Resistance.}
Hoffmann~\cite{hoffmann2021security} provides a quantitative security analysis of the ceremony mechanism. The key finding is that an adversary must control a supermajority of reputables within a single meetup group to create a Sybil identity---specifically, $(g - 2)$ out of $g$ reputables, where $g$ is the group size. At group sizes of 12, this requires controlling 10 of 12 participants in a group, making the probability of success negligible below 50\% adversarial control of the overall network.

The analysis identifies a \emph{friction threshold} of approximately 4.5\%: if the cost of recruiting a ceremony minion exceeds 4.5\% of the ceremony reward, no Sybil strategy is profitable within a two-year horizon. In practice, the ceremony reward of approximately \$2 combined with the logistical costs of coordinating physical attendance creates effective friction well above this threshold, particularly in communities where participants know each other.

Ford~\cite{ford2020identity} critiques ceremony-based PoP by modeling small groups of 4 members. However, Hoffmann's analysis---conducted under Ford's own supervision---corrects this by analyzing the actual group sizes of 3--12, showing substantially stronger security guarantees. The puppeteering risks documented by Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} for the Idena protocol, where operators paid \$2--\$14 per session for remote participants' keys, are structurally mitigated by the physical co-presence requirement: ceremony minions must physically travel to assigned locations, raising coordination costs and social detection risk.

\paragraph{Reputation Model.}
Successful ceremony attendance earns a \emph{reputation} valid for a rolling window of $R = 5$ ceremonies. Participants progress through three statuses: \emph{Newbie} (first attendance), \emph{Reputable} (at least one prior successful ceremony), and \emph{Bootstrapper} (community founder with elevated trust). Reputation is non-transferable and decays automatically---a participant who stops attending loses all reputation within 5 cycles. This creates a \emph{liveness} requirement that continuously validates personhood, unlike one-time identity verification schemes.

\section{Democratic Governance Protocol}
\label{sec:governance}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Open Proposal Submission}
\label{sec:proposals}

Any participant with reputable status can submit a governance proposal on-chain without requiring approval from existing authorities. This permissionless design prevents gatekeeping---a common failure mode in DAO governance where proposal submission is restricted to token holders above a threshold, effectively silencing smaller stakeholders~\cite{schneider2024governable}.

Each proposal specifies a \emph{ProposalAction} drawn from a fixed set of governance operations: modifying community parameters (nominal income, demurrage rate, ceremony locations), spending treasury funds, issuing swap options, or submitting non-binding petitions. Proposals are either community-scoped (only members of that community vote) or global (all communities vote). The electorate is computed at submission time and remains fixed throughout the proposal's lifetime, ensuring a stable approval threshold.

\subsection{Parallel Competing Proposals}
\label{sec:parallel}

Multiple proposals of the same type can coexist simultaneously. For parameter-setting actions (e.g., two competing proposals to set different demurrage rates), the first proposal to reach approval supersedes all older competing proposals, which transition to a \emph{SupersededBy} state. This first-to-pass mechanism prevents deadlocks where conflicting proposals block each other indefinitely.

Non-parameter actions---treasury spends, swap option grants, and petitions---do not supersede each other, allowing multiple independent spending proposals to proceed in parallel. This distinction reflects the difference between mutually exclusive parameter choices and additive resource allocations.

\subsection{Participation-Weighted Voting Power}
\label{sec:voting-power}

Rather than assigning equal voting power to all eligible participants, the protocol weights votes by ceremony participation history. A voter's \emph{voting power} equals their reputation count within the eligible window for that proposal---the number of ceremonies successfully attended in recent cycles.

Formally, for a proposal submitted at ceremony index $c_s$, the eligible ceremony indices are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:voting-window}
\mathcal{C} = \{c_s - R + L_p, \ldots, c_s - 2\}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the reputation lifetime (5 ceremonies) and $L_p$ is the proposal lifetime in cycles. A voter's power is $v = |\{c \in \mathcal{C} : \text{voter attended ceremony } c\}|$, capped at $R - 1 = 4$.

The upper bound of the eligible window ($c_s - 2$) excludes the most recent ceremony, creating a one-cycle delay. This prevents last-minute Sybil infiltration: an attacker cannot register new identities and immediately deploy them in a governance vote. The lower bound ensures that the oldest eligible reputations still exist at the end of the proposal lifetime, guaranteeing a stable electorate throughout the voting period.

The total electorate $E$ is the sum of reputation counts across all eligible ceremonies for the relevant community (or globally, for global proposals):
\begin{equation}
E = \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \text{reputationCount}(c)
\end{equation}

This design rewards sustained participation: a voter attending all ceremonies has 4$\times$ the influence of a voter attending only once. The cap at $4\times$ bounds inequality---in contrast to token-weighted voting where power differences can span orders of magnitude. Inactive participants lose voting power automatically as their reputations expire, keeping the electorate current.

Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} found that Idena's analogous mechanism---one-pool-one-vote, which discounted influence for concentrated account pools---was decisive in enabling the protocol to pivot away from captured governance. Our participation weighting serves a similar function: it makes sustained, genuine engagement a prerequisite for governance influence.

\subsection{Adaptive Quorum Biasing}
\label{sec:quorum}

A fixed majority threshold (e.g., 50\%) creates a legitimacy problem under variable turnout: a proposal passing with 51\% of a 5\% turnout represents support from only 2.5\% of the electorate. Conversely, requiring high absolute turnout causes governance paralysis when participation fluctuates.

We adopt \emph{adaptive quorum biasing} (AQB) with a positive turnout bias. Let $E$ denote the electorate size, $T$ the total voting power of all voters (turnout), and $A$ the voting power of aye voters. The proposal passes if:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:aqb}
\frac{T - A}{\sqrt{T}} < \frac{A}{\sqrt{E}}
\end{equation}
which yields an approval threshold as a fraction of turnout:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:threshold}
\theta(T, E) = \frac{\sqrt{E}}{\sqrt{E} + \sqrt{T}}
\end{equation}

At full turnout ($T = E$), the threshold is exactly $50\%$---a simple majority suffices. As turnout decreases, $\theta$ increases toward $100\%$, requiring near-unanimity among the few who vote. This encodes a reasonable democratic principle: decisions affecting the entire community should require broad consensus when most members are absent, but a simple majority suffices when everyone participates.

Additionally, a \emph{MinTurnout} floor (expressed in per mille of the electorate) prevents proposals from passing when turnout is negligibly small, regardless of unanimity among voters.

A proposal that meets the AQB threshold enters a \emph{Confirming} state. It must remain continuously passing for a confirmation period before transitioning to \emph{Approved} and entering the enactment queue. If the threshold is no longer met (e.g., due to new nay votes), the proposal returns to \emph{Ongoing}. If a proposal is not approved within its lifetime, it is \emph{Rejected}.

\section{Treasury Swap Governance for Community Currencies}
\label{sec:treasury}
%=====================================================================

Community currencies face a recurring sustainability challenge: popular businesses that attract many customers accumulate tokens faster than they can spend them within the local network. Ruddick~\cite{ruddick2022grassroots} documents this ``accumulation problem'' across over 100 community currency deployments in Kenya, where businesses with excess tokens lose motivation to continue accepting the currency, threatening network viability.

\paragraph{Swap Options as Democratic Policy.}
Our architecture addresses this through \emph{treasury swap options}: a community can democratically vote to grant a specific business the right to exchange accumulated community currency tokens for stablecoins (e.g., USDC) held in the community treasury. Each swap option is defined by:
\begin{itemize}
\item A beneficiary address (the business receiving swap rights)
\item An allowance (maximum stablecoin amount available for swap)
\item An exchange rate (community currency units per stablecoin unit)
\item A burn flag (whether swapped community currency is burned or returned to treasury)
\item Optional validity window (start and end dates)
\end{itemize}

To exercise a swap option, the beneficiary sends community currency tokens to the treasury and receives stablecoins up to their remaining allowance. Each exercise reduces the allowance, preventing unlimited extraction. If the burn flag is set, the swapped community currency is permanently destroyed, reducing circulating supply; otherwise, it returns to the treasury for future distribution.

\paragraph{Safeguards Against Treasury Depletion.}
Two community-level conventions protect treasuries from excessive extraction. First, a \emph{per-beneficiary limit} caps each swap option at 10\% of the treasury balance, ensuring no single business can drain a disproportionate share. Second, a 2\% \emph{exercise fee} is deducted from each swap, creating a cost of extraction that discourages speculative or unnecessary swaps. Both parameters are themselves subject to democratic governance---communities can adjust them through the standard proposal mechanism.

The critical design insight is that \emph{the community itself} decides who receives swap access and under what terms. Unlike centralized models where an administrator or foundation controls treasury outflows, every swap option must pass the adaptive quorum biasing threshold described in Section~\ref{sec:quorum}. This creates democratic accountability: businesses must maintain community trust to continue receiving swap access.

The primary deployment is Community~A in East Africa, where approximately 60 entrepreneurs---tailors, food vendors, and cosmetics sellers---participate in regular ceremonies. Community~B in West Africa has grown rapidly since its launch, reaching 47 participants at peak. Community~C in Europe served as an early testbed but has since declined in active participation.

Over the observation period (December 2024 to March 2026), the governance system processed 67 proposals: 27 swap option grants, 23 native treasury spends, 11 petitions, 3 nominal income updates, 2 demurrage adjustments, and 1 location addition. Of these, 58 were approved and enacted, 6 were rejected (all due to expiration with zero votes), 2 were superseded by competing proposals, and 1 remains in confirming state.

Figure~\ref{fig:aqb} visualizes the adaptive quorum biasing mechanism against empirical proposal data. The theoretical threshold curve shows the required approval fraction as a function of turnout: at low turnout, near-unanimity is required; at full turnout, a simple majority suffices.

\begin{figure}[t]

Of the 63 proposals that received at least one vote, the mean turnout was 11.5\% of the electorate and the mean approval rate among voters was 96.4\%. All voted-upon proposals achieved approval rates well above the AQB threshold at their respective turnout levels, resulting in enactment. The 6 rejected proposals all expired with zero turnout---no proposal has been actively voted down with nay votes exceeding the threshold.

This pattern suggests two interpretations. Optimistically, the community achieves strong consensus before bringing proposals to a vote, and the AQB mechanism correctly interprets non-participation as implicit consent. Alternatively, the lack of contested proposals may reflect a community too small or too homogeneous for meaningful disagreement to emerge. We consider this a limitation: the AQB mechanism has not yet been stress-tested under adversarial or genuinely contested conditions.

Of these 22 granted swap options, 14 have been successfully exercised (12 in Community~A, 1 each in Communities B and C), indicating active utilization of the mechanism. The per-beneficiary limit of 10\% of treasury balance and the 2\% exercise fee have not yet been tested under conditions of treasury scarcity. Current utilization levels suggest the mechanism is functioning as intended---providing democratic oversight of treasury outflows while enabling businesses to convert excess community currency to stablecoins.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=====================================================================

\paragraph{Democratic Governance Without Plutocracy.}
Our architecture demonstrates that DAOs can govern shared treasury resources through one-person-one-vote mechanisms when a reliable PoP layer is available. The ceremony-based approach sidesteps the privacy and centralization concerns of biometric systems while providing Sybil resistance that, per Hoffmann's analysis~\cite{hoffmann2021security}, is robust below the 50\% adversarial threshold---a condition easily maintained in small-to-medium communities with low ceremony rewards. The participation-weighted voting mechanism further strengthens governance integrity by rewarding sustained engagement over one-time participation.

\paragraph{Participation and Legitimacy.}
Adaptive quorum biasing provides a principled mechanism for navigating the tension between governance agility and democratic legitimacy under variable turnout. Our empirical findings show that proposals consistently achieve high approval rates among those who vote, while the AQB threshold ensures that low-turnout decisions require near-unanimity. The observation that all rejected proposals expired with zero turnout---rather than being actively voted down---suggests that non-participation functions as a form of implicit consent or indifference, which AQB appropriately handles by raising the bar for passage.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations constrain the generalizability of our findings. First, the dataset is small: 67 proposals across two active communities over approximately 16 months. Second, no proposal has been actively contested with significant nay votes---the AQB mechanism has not yet been tested under adversarial conditions. Third, the PoP ceremony mechanism introduces participation burden: attending physical meetups every 10 days is demanding, and ceremony attendance may be influenced by facilitators who help coordinate logistics, introducing an informal power dynamic not captured in the on-chain governance model. Fourth, the economic value of community currencies remains small (ceremony rewards of approximately \$2), which provides natural Sybil resistance through high effective friction but also limits the stakes of governance decisions. Whether the architecture maintains its democratic properties as economic value grows remains an open question.

\paragraph{Generalizability.}
The governance protocol is not specific to community currencies. Any DAO with a PoP identity layer could adopt the adaptive quorum biasing, participation-weighted voting, and parallel proposal mechanisms described here. The treasury swap governance application is domain-specific, but the underlying pattern---democratic oversight of resource allocation with per-beneficiary caps---applies broadly to grant programs, public goods funding, and other collective resource management scenarios. The per-community structure, which Ohlhaver et al.~\cite{ohlhaver2024proof} identify as structurally advantageous for Sybil resistance through subsidiarity, also enables governance customization: communities can set their own parameters while sharing infrastructure.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We presented a field-deployed governance architecture that enables democratic treasury management in DAOs without plutocratic token-weighted voting. By combining ceremony-based proof-of-personhood with participation-weighted voting and adaptive quorum biasing, the system achieves one-person-one-vote governance while maintaining agility under variable turnout. Applied to treasury swap governance in community currencies, the architecture allows communities to democratically manage stablecoin-backed swap options with per-beneficiary caps and exercise fees. Empirical analysis from deployments in two African communities demonstrates sustained participation, high governance activity, and effective quorum dynamics.

Future work should examine contested proposal dynamics as communities grow, cross-community governance mechanisms for shared resources, and privacy-preserving voting through ring-VRF-based anonymous credentials. Larger deployments will provide stronger evidence on whether ceremony-based PoP scales to communities where participants are not personally acquainted, and whether the democratic properties of the governance architecture hold as the economic value of community currencies increases.
