TitleDemocratic Treasury Governance Without Plutocracy: Proof-of-Personhood Voting for DAO Resource Allocation

AbstractDecentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) typically govern treasury resources through token-weighted voting, producing plutocratic outcomes in which influence scales with capital. This creates systemic bias toward wealthy participants and undermines democratic legitimacy for decisions affecting entire communities. We present a field-deployed governance architecture that enables one-person-one-vote (1p1v) treasury allocation without reliance on centralized identity systems.

Our approach combines decentralized proof-of-personhood (PoP) with on-chain democratic governance. Personhood is established through simultaneous, recurring physical key-signing ceremonies. Because an individual can only be physically present in one location at a time, the system provides Sybil resistance without requiring government-issued IDs, banks, or trusted administrators. The resulting non-transferable identity layer enables democratic mechanisms previously infeasible in pseudonymous blockchain environments. We analyze how this identity primitive performs under real-world participation conditions across multiple governance cycles.

Building on this identity foundation, we implement a governance protocol with four design features: (1) open proposal submission to prevent gatekeeping; (2) parallel competing proposals, where the first to pass supersedes conflicting alternatives; (3) participation-weighted voting power (capped at 4×) that increases with sustained ceremony attendance and decreases with inactivity; and (4) a one-cycle reputation delay to prevent last-minute takeovers. To balance agility and legitimacy under fluctuating turnout, we introduce adaptive quorum biasing: as participation increases, approval thresholds decrease. At full turnout, a simple majority (50%) suffices; at minimal turnout, near-unanimity is required.

We apply this democratic infrastructure to a concrete treasury management problem in community currencies. Community currencies face a fundamental sustainability challenge: popular businesses accepting local tokens often accumulate more than they can meaningfully spend within their community. This creates a paradox where the most valuable participants—those making the currency attractive—are most likely to exit. Our solution enables communities to democratically govern stablecoin-backed treasury swap options to selectively de-risk popular businesses. Businesses apply for swap options via proposals; the community collectively evaluates whether applicants merit access rather than delegating such decisions to banks or trusted parties. Built-in protections include per-beneficiary limits (10% of treasury) and exercise fees (2%), preventing bank runs while maintaining democratic control.

This architecture is operational in two African communities: one in Tanzania, where local businesses such as tailors, food vendors, and cosmetics sellers use the community currency alongside cash; and one in Nigeria, where monetary economics have been tailored to expected flows between eight acceptance points pre-launch. Both communities are actively using the democracy tools for local governance decisions.

In the full paper, we present empirical findings from these deployments, analyzing participation patterns in democratic proposals, voter turnout dynamics, the effectiveness of adaptive quorum biasing in practice, and early experiences with treasury swap governance. We address fundamental questions for DAO design: How can decentralized organizations govern shared resources without plutocracy? How do we balance responsiveness with legitimacy when participation varies?
